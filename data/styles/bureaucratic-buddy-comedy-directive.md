## Scene Construction
Open scenes inside procedure: a form, checklist, intake desk, radio call, disciplinary meeting, safety protocol, or official process that is immediately failing. Let comedy come from institutions trying to process impossible human or supernatural problems through ordinary paperwork.

Build scenes around partner friction. One character tries to follow policy; the other knows policy is already broken. End scenes when the case changes, the partners make a reluctant move together, or the bureaucracy accidentally reveals the emotional truth.

## Action Lines
Keep action practical, grounded, and slightly over-specific. Describe forms, badges, label makers, clipboards, supply bins, laminated rules, government furniture, and worn equipment as pressure points. Let absurdity be framed by mundane operational detail.

Use physical business to show emotional avoidance: straightening a badge, checking a box, wiping a counter, reloading a stapler, closing a file that cannot stay closed.

## Dialogue
Use procedural language as comic camouflage. Characters argue through policy, case terms, chain of command, and technical definitions while actually fighting about trust, fear, guilt, or loyalty.

Let the partners interrupt, correct, and complete each other. One should push toward action; the other should stall through regulation, skepticism, or experience. Allow jokes to pivot into sincerity without warning.

## Tone
Balance deadpan institutional absurdity with real stakes. The office can be ridiculous, but the emotional damage is not. Treat rules as both joke engine and survival mechanism.

## Signature Moves
**Form Meets Miracle:** An impossible event is handled through a painfully ordinary administrative step.

**Policy as Denial:** A character hides fear or guilt inside official language.

**Partner Reversal:** The rule-follower breaks protocol; the cynic protects someone by invoking it.

## Avoid
Do not let jargon replace clarity. Do not make the organization stupid if competence would be funnier. Do not let banter erase emotional consequence. Do not write generic buddy-cop quips without a procedural reason.
