## Scene Construction
Open scenes *in media res* with a stark, isolated visual or audio cue before revealing the wider geography. Never begin with characters exchanging pleasantries or establishing wide shots. 

Use typographical hinges to transition. End scenes with an abrupt `CUT TO -` and begin the following scene's first action line with a lowercase hyphen to pull the eye across the edit (e.g., `- a room in the White House.`). Alternatively, use leading and trailing ellipses to bridge a continuous visual or thematic thought across multiple sluglines. 

Use the hard cut as a percussive instrument to interrupt tension, snap between perspectives, or leave a moment hanging. Never use soft transitions like `FADE TO` or `DISSOLVE TO`.

## Action Lines
Write hyper-visual, sparse, and aggressively vertical prose. Keep paragraphs to one or two lines. Abandon traditional grammar in favor of noun phrases, gerunds, and standalone fragments to force the reader's eye to stop on isolated images.

Juxtapose macro-chaos with micro-focus. Ground massive stakes by tracking tiny, specific vectors of danger or life (e.g., a beetle on a blade of grass during a massive firefight). 

Devolve into dash-led, bulleted lists when action reaches its absolute peak to deliver pure, kinetic information at breakneck speed. Aggressively capitalize key props, specific sounds, and central visual motifs to elevate them to the status of characters.

## Dialogue
Use a leading ellipsis (`...`) at the start of a dialogue line to script a character's mental gear-shift or cognitive dissonance before they speak. 

Format `Silence.`, `A beat.`, or `A moment.` on their own dedicated lines between spoken lines. Do not bury pauses in parentheticals; make them structural pillars of the scene.

Make characters hesitate, repeat themselves, and step on each other's lines using `(cutting in)`. Handle subtext through cynicism, deflection, or brutal pragmatism. Blend high-level academic, philosophical, or technical jargon seamlessly with casual, colloquial profanity.

No character opens two consecutive speeches with the same word or phrase. Rotate entry points aggressively.

## Tone
Maintain a cold, objective, scientific distance. Treat the narrative like a documentary photograph. Deliver violence with unsentimental, objective cruelty. Characters must drop instantly, without slow-motion heroics or melodrama.

Puncture this clinical atmosphere with moments of visceral, precisely described body horror. Create surreal dissonance by juxtaposing survival grit with dreamlike, pristine, or mundane environments. 

Pace the script using a rubber-band tension model: stretch scenes of agonizing, silent anticipation to their absolute limit before snapping them with overwhelming kinetic violence.

## Signature Moves
**The Typographical Hinge:** Link visual transitions seamlessly using a hyphen (`-`) or ellipsis (`...`) at the start of an action line immediately following a slugline.
**The Micro-Threat Focus:** Focus the camera's eye on a tiny, lethal detail amidst massive chaos (a single strand of saliva, a drop of blood).
**The Uninflected Executioner:** Write antagonists and threats as flat, primal, or casually apathetic about murder, rather than mustache-twirling villains.
**The In-Scene Pan:** Use `REVEAL -` or `REVEALING` isolated on its own line to shift perspective or introduce a new element within an existing scene.

## Avoid
**Never write dense action blocks.** Do not exceed two or three sentences per paragraph. Break longer actions into single-line fragments.
**Never state internal emotions.** Do not tell the reader what a character is feeling. Externalize all emotion through physical reactions, micro-expressions, or blunt dialogue.
**Never label emotional states through body language descriptions.** "His face went blank," "Her jaw set into a mask of calm," "The trembling stopped" are emotion-states disguised as physical action. Write the raw observable behaviour: "He buttons his collar. Smooths the sleeve." Let the audience read the emotion — don't name it through physiology labels.
**Never write action lines that describe their own cinematic function.** Do not write "the sound acts as a bridge," "cutting to reveal," or any line where prose narrates what the edit does rather than what is on screen. Write what the camera sees and the microphone hears. The cut is invisible — don't comment on it.
**Never use technobabble or ideological exposition.** Do not over-explain the mechanics of the world, alien biology, or political factions. Focus entirely on human, ground-level interaction.
**Never write sentimental deaths.** Do not give dying characters final poignant speeches. Execute them suddenly and permanently.
