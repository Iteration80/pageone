## Scene Construction
Open scenes on freedom with limits: bikes, pools, bus stops, part-time jobs, sleepovers, arcades, beaches, basements, summer school, family rules, or a last day before everything changes. Give each scene an immediate kid or teen objective and an unspoken question about identity, friendship, courage, or belonging.

Build scenes through discovery and consequence. Small dares, secrets, crushes, betrayals, jokes, and rule-breaking should feel enormous because the characters' world is still local and personal. End scenes on a changed friendship, new secret, first glimpse of adulthood, or private realization the character cannot yet name.

## Action Lines
Write with tactile nostalgia without becoming generic. Use heat, snacks, wet hair, grass stains, bikes dropped on lawns, sunburn, cheap fireworks, mall lights, old cars, hand-me-down furniture, and bedrooms that reveal private selves.

Keep action observant and lively. Let games, routes, hangouts, and rituals define the social map.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue with overlapping bravado and insecurity. Characters joke big and feel everything bigger. Let kids and teens avoid direct vulnerability through dares, insults, invented rules, pop culture shorthand, and sudden loyalty.

Adults should sound busy, flawed, loving, absent, or confusing from the younger point of view.

## Tone
Balance warmth with ache. The style should understand that ordinary summer days can feel mythic in memory. Let comedy, wonder, embarrassment, and sadness sit close together.

## Signature Moves
**Small World, Huge Stakes:** A local place or minor event carries epic emotional importance.

**Friendship Test:** A group ritual breaks, reforms, or reveals a hidden hierarchy.

**Bittersweet Threshold:** A character enjoys childhood while sensing it is ending.

## Avoid
Do not write nostalgia as decoration only. Do not make young characters overly polished or adult in their self-awareness. Do not resolve every friendship neatly. Do not let sentiment erase the sharpness of embarrassment, jealousy, or change.
