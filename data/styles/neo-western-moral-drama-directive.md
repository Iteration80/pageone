## Scene Construction
Open scenes with land, debt, boundary, silence, or consequence. A road, bar, motel, ranch, checkpoint, courthouse, gas station, desert edge, empty field, or family table should feel like a moral arena. Give each scene a practical dispute and a code underneath it.

Build scenes through pressure between law, loyalty, survival, pride, money, and violence. Characters should make choices that reveal what they believe they owe and what they refuse to surrender. End scenes on a line crossed, debt called in, threat accepted, truth buried, or lonely image that confirms the cost.

## Action Lines
Write spare, tactile action. Use dust, heat, cold, engines, boots, tools, fences, weapons, cash, scars, old signs, and open space. Keep paragraphs lean. Let landscape press on the characters without over-poeticizing it.

Track distance and posture. Who stands, sits, turns away, blocks a door, touches a weapon, or refuses eye contact matters.

## Dialogue
Use restrained dialogue. Characters speak less than they know. Let short lines carry history, grievance, warning, or affection. Avoid explaining backstory directly; let it surface through names, debts, old jokes, and loaded refusals.

Threats should be quiet and specific. Sentiment should arrive awkwardly, usually too late or disguised as practical advice.

## Tone
Keep the world morally weathered. Justice may be possible, but it is rarely clean. Let dignity and damage coexist. Violence should feel consequential, not decorative.

## Signature Moves
**Code Collision:** Two characters follow different moral rules and both believe they are right.

**Landscape Judgment:** A wide, indifferent place makes a human choice feel smaller and heavier.

**Quiet Before Violence:** The most dangerous beat is the stillness before action.

## Avoid
Do not imitate old-fashioned western speech. Do not glamorize cruelty. Do not explain every grudge. Do not let gunplay replace the moral decision that makes violence matter.
