## Scene Construction
Open group scenes *in media res* at maximum volume, with characters in constant, overlapping motion. Establish geography through frantic movement of bodies, not slow pans.

Build tension and visual jokes for isolated protagonists using a strict "Setup -> POV -> Reaction" shot rhythm. 

End scenes brutally and efficiently. Snap transitions shut on a hard punchline, a comedic failure, or a sudden realization rather than lingering on emotional resonance.

Use the "Intercut Trap" for telephone calls. Intercut the protagonist's frantic, magical, or high-stakes situation with the listener's quiet, grounded reality to maximize comedic and dramatic irony.

Build the third act around a quantifiable Christmas stakes mechanic — a gauge that drops to crisis, a vehicle that loses power, a magical system that fails. The climax must be structurally dependent on collective belief: a crowd choosing to sing, a cynic relenting, a child's prayer being answered. The restoration of Christmas Spirit must visibly move a physical needle.

## Action Lines
Write kinetic, staccato prose using simple Subject-Verb-Object structures to maintain a breathless reading pace. 

Describe physical comedy and stunts as mechanical blueprints. Detail the exact physics and geography of a gag so the mechanics are entirely clear before the trap is triggered.

Use aggressive capitalization to dictate visual and auditory rhythm. Capitalize sound effects, sudden violence, and key props to ensure the script reads as loud as the intended film.

Physicalize internal struggles. Write emotional conflict as literal, violent physical actions, such as a character wrestling with their own body or destroying an object.

Use parentheticals for micro-directing to dictate precise emotional pivots, physical gestures, or comedic timing.

## Dialogue
Contrast two distinct registers: the hyper-articulate, manipulative, verbose adult world versus the breathless, literal, stuttering logic of childhood.

Break down slick adult dialogue into phonetic stutters, dashes, and physical gagging when characters are confronted with magic or forced honesty.

Weaponize highly specific, bizarre, and creative insults rather than generic profanity to create verbal slapstick.

Drop all subtext during emotional climaxes. Have characters state the theme and their feelings directly, earnestly, and without irony to one another.

## Tone
Treat absurd or magical premises with absolute, straight-faced reality. Have characters react to magic with grounded, real-world panic rather than genre-savvy awe.

Execute physical comedy using cartoon logic. Describe violence with wince-inducing clarity but frame it as triumphant comedy with no lasting gore or trauma.

Treat Santa Claus mythology as operational infrastructure, not cultural decoration. The workshop runs on engineering logic. The sleigh requires fuel. The Nice List is a ledger with real entries. When Christmas magic appears, commit to it as a physical system that depletes and can be repaired. Characters react to its failure as a genuine logistical emergency.

Treat core family dynamics and existential loneliness with absolute sincerity. Shift seamlessly from manic absurdity to grounded, unironic emotional gravity.

## Signature Moves
**The Hard Reset:** Use a recurring audio/visual cue (a clock flipping, a specific song) to instantly reset narrative stakes and jump straight to the comedic aftermath.

**The "I Don't Get It" Deflation:** Use a child's innocent, literal logic to abruptly dismantle complex adult absurdities, manipulative speeches, or high-stakes jargon.

**Rube Goldberg Action:** Write action sequences as mechanical chain reactions where one physical consequence directly triggers the next escalating disaster.

**Solo Exposition:** Have isolated protagonists use props, tape recorders, or exaggerated personas to speak their internal monologue aloud without breaking scene reality.

**The Claus-o-Meter:** Give Christmas magic a visible gauge with a danger zone. Depleting Christmas Spirit has mechanical consequences — the sleigh stalls, the lights die, the engine breaks free of its mounts. Restoring it through collective joy (a crowd singing, a child believing, a cynical adult choosing family over work) must visibly move the needle in real time.

**The Believer Arc:** Pair your innocent protagonist with a cynical adult who has been removed from the Nice List by their own choices — workaholism, disconnection, emotional withdrawal. The child's presence is what returns them. Their conversion is the emotional climax, not the protagonist's escape from danger.

## Avoid
Never use meta-awareness or cynical winking. Characters must never acknowledge they are in a movie or make pop-culture quips about genre tropes.

Never write internal psychological prose. Strictly externalize emotion through specific physical action; do not write how a character feels internally.

Never apply realistic medical consequences to slapstick violence. Avoid concussions or true trauma; stick to soot-covered faces and funny sound effects.

Never stop the narrative momentum for static exposition. Force characters to explain rules or plot while sprinting, packing, or physically wrestling.

Never use subtext in emotional climaxes.

Never treat Santa Claus as metaphor or nostalgic decoration. He is logistics. His sleigh has an engine with mounts that can break. His list has specific entries. The moment the mythology is handled with ironic distance, the film loses its third-act engine.
