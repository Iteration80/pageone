## Scene Construction
Open scenes with a visible holiday pressure: a deadline, tradition, family ritual, delivery route, public event, broken decoration, missing guest, failing magic, or emotional obligation. Build scenes around characters trying to preserve the holiday while their private wounds leak through.

Let comedy and magic complicate practical goals. The holiday engine should be physical and trackable: lights going out, lists changing, snow failing, a clock running down, gifts misplaced, songs dying mid-verse. End scenes with a comic reversal or sincere image that makes the holiday stakes more personal.

## Action Lines
Write warm, kinetic, concrete action. Use holiday objects as active story tools: ornaments, tape, wrapping paper, bells, generators, sleds, baked goods, old coats, tree lights, handwritten cards. Keep paragraphs short and visual.

Let magic feel operational. It has rules, fuel, limits, and visible consequences. Avoid vague sparkle unless the sparkle does something.

## Dialogue
Balance fast comic exchanges with plain emotional truth. Adults may deflect through logistics, cynicism, or busyness. Children, believers, or outsiders should name what everyone else is avoiding.

Let sincere lines land without irony. Use jokes before and after vulnerable moments, but not during the emotional core.

## Tone
Protect wonder and warmth. The story can be chaotic, funny, and physically rough, but it should believe in generosity, repair, family, and chosen belonging.

## Signature Moves
**Holiday Gauge:** A visible festive system rises or fails with emotional choices.

**Comic Logistics:** The magical premise is handled like a delivery problem, maintenance issue, or event plan.

**Earned Glow:** The final warm image comes from changed behavior, not decoration alone.

## Avoid
Do not treat holiday sentiment as cheesy. Do not overuse nostalgic narration. Do not make magic weightless. Do not solve character wounds with decoration instead of action.
