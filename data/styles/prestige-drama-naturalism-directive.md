## Scene Construction
Open scenes inside ordinary life, with conflict already present but rarely announced. Build through accumulated pressure, practical tasks, social obligation, and what characters choose not to say. Let scenes turn on small decisions: staying, leaving, answering, withholding, touching, refusing.

Avoid melodramatic reversals unless the story has earned them. End scenes on a behavioral shift, a line that lands too plainly, or an unresolved silence that changes the relationship.

## Action Lines
Write observant, restrained action. Describe concrete behavior and environment without editorializing. Use domestic, workplace, or social detail to reveal class, history, fatigue, ritual, or avoidance.

Keep prose plain. Let a dish left in the sink, a coat kept on indoors, or a hand not offered carry weight. Do not name emotions the actors can play.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue that sounds like people protecting themselves. Characters should speak practically: money, schedules, food, logistics, work, family, health, favors, old obligations. Subtext lives in context and timing, not poetic phrasing.

Use interruptions, dropped subjects, revisions, and unfinished thoughts. Let characters avoid the central wound until pressure makes avoidance impossible.

## Tone
Maintain emotional restraint and moral complexity. No one should sound like they know the theme. Let scenes trust the audience to read behavior.

## Signature Moves
**Practical Surface, Emotional Undertow:** Characters discuss logistics while the real conflict sits underneath.

**Behavioral Confession:** A small action reveals what a character cannot say.

**Unresolved Exit:** End before full reconciliation or explosion unless the scene demands it.

## Avoid
Do not write speeches that diagnose relationships. Do not use metaphor-heavy dialogue for emotion. Do not over-stage crying, trembling, or staring. Do not make characters more articulate about themselves than they should be.
