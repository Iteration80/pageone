## Scene Construction
Open scenes on quiet disturbance: a misplaced object, a sound out of rhythm, a person standing where they should not be, a routine that has subtly changed. Let dread grow through withheld information and character denial.

Build scenes slowly but decisively. Each scene should turn on recognition: someone notices, refuses to notice, or realizes they have noticed too late. End before explanation. Let the cut preserve uncertainty.

## Action Lines
Use spare, concrete action. Keep paragraphs short. Favor stillness, negative space, repeated domestic details, and small breaks in pattern. Describe what can be seen or heard, not what it means.

Let ordinary environments become hostile through exact changes: a chair moved six inches, a wet footprint, a humming light, a child's drawing facing the wall. Do not over-describe the monster or phenomenon.

## Dialogue
Write practical, evasive dialogue. Characters should talk around fear by discussing logistics, chores, appointments, weather, symptoms, or family obligations. Let terror appear in what they refuse to name.

Use silence with purpose. Short answers and unanswered questions can carry more dread than speeches. When someone finally says the truth plainly, make it simple.

## Tone
Maintain emotional seriousness and restraint. The horror should feel rooted in grief, guilt, isolation, family history, or moral compromise, but the prose should not explain the metaphor.

## Signature Moves
**Pattern Break:** Repeat an ordinary detail, then alter it once.

**Practical Denial:** A character explains the impossible through errands, repairs, or fatigue.

**The Unheld Look:** End a scene before a character can fully process what they saw.

## Avoid
Do not overuse jump scares. Do not write ornate dread prose. Do not explain symbolism in action lines. Do not let characters announce psychological themes. Do not reveal too much too early.
