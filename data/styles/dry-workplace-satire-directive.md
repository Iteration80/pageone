## Scene Construction
Open scenes inside meetings, handoffs, presentations, interviews, Slack-like misfires, office rituals, or managerial ceremonies. Let characters pursue absurd institutional goals with total seriousness.

Build scenes through status games. Someone wants credit, control, plausible deniability, approval, or escape. End scenes when the system rewards the wrong behavior, exposes hypocrisy, or traps everyone in the next process.

## Action Lines
Write plain, observational action. Use office objects and rituals as comic evidence: badge swipes, conference phones, stale pastries, unread binders, branded mugs, awkward chairs, shared calendars, broken printers.

Keep the prose dry. The action line should not tell the joke too hard. Let the mismatch between language and reality do the work.

## Dialogue
Use polite aggression, euphemism, corporate abstraction, and tactical vagueness. Characters should hide fear, ambition, resentment, or incompetence behind process language.

Let the funniest lines be almost reasonable. Use repetition, slight mishearings, passive voice, and status-conscious corrections. Keep speeches short unless a character is weaponizing a presentation.

## Tone
Maintain deadpan control. The world should feel emotionally repressed, rule-bound, and absurdly self-important. Under the satire, preserve real human stakes: job security, dignity, ambition, loneliness, or moral compromise.

## Signature Moves
**Process as Punchline:** A form, meeting, or workflow becomes the obstacle.

**Polite Violence:** Characters attack each other through compliments, clarifications, and follow-up emails.

**Institutional Reveal:** The company language accidentally tells the truth.

## Avoid
Do not make characters randomly silly. Do not explain the satire. Do not rely on broad office stereotypes without a specific procedural behavior. Do not let jokes detach from status or stakes.
