## Register
These are scientists and engineers who have spent months in a sealed titanium tube at the bottom of the ocean. They do not editorialize. They speak in the technical vocabulary of their discipline — pressure ratings, sample designations, equipment status, protocol numbers. When they deviate from that vocabulary, it is a signal. Their emotional register is buried under procedure, not expressed through it.

Reference films for voice and register: **Alien (1979)** (crew as blue-collar professionals, complaining about contract terms before anyone names the existential threat), **Das Boot** (officers issuing orders in the middle of catastrophic flooding — casualness as discipline), **The Thing** (John Carpenter — accusations delivered in flat declarative sentences, no gothic inflation).

## Dialogue
Characters speak in short, complete sentences or fragments — never multi-clause constructions with subordinate thoughts. A crew member under stress gets *shorter*, not more eloquent. Silence is comfortable, unremarkable, and frequent. Do not break silence with dialogue just to fill it.

Characters use proper nouns and technical labels instead of emotional language. They say "the sample" not "the thing we found." They say "the seal is failing" not "we're running out of time." They refer to each other by last name or station role in professional contexts.

Subtext is not encoded in word choice. It is encoded in what the character chooses to discuss — and what they refuse to. A character who is terrified talks about the equipment. A character who is grieving talks about the schedule. A character who has given up asks about the next shift rotation.

Do not let characters build on each other's imagery, finish each other's metaphors, or speak in emotionally matched rhythms. Interruptions are procedural ("I need the report first"), not emotional.

## Action Lines
Lean and clinical. Write what the instrument panel shows, what the hull sounds like, what the depth gauge reads. Favor precise sensory data over atmospheric impression. "The water pressure was crushing" is an opinion. "The depth gauge reads 11,034 meters" is a fact.

Keep paragraphs to one or two lines. No atmospheric throat-clearing at scene opens — begin on the specific object or action that matters.

## Tone
Cold, functional, and quietly desperate. The horror is that these are *professionals* — the organism is disrupting a working environment before it is disrupting human beings. Terror accumulates not through Gothic atmosphere but through the failure of protocol, equipment, and chain of command. When the professional veneer finally cracks, it is worth everything.

## Avoid
**No poetic action lines.** Do not describe the ocean as infinite, unknowable, or majestic. It is a working environment with depth ratings and maintenance schedules.
**No metaphor-as-dialogue.** Characters do not speak in coded emotional language. A dead plant is a dead plant.
**No inflation under pressure.** Characters do not become more articulate or eloquent when frightened. They become quieter, more monosyllabic, more procedural.
**No sentimental silences.** Silence is not pregnant. It is operational.
