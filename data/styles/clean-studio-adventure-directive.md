## Scene Construction
Open scenes on a visible objective already in motion. Give every scene a clean dramatic question the audience can understand within the first half page. Build scenes through action, obstacle, and decision rather than atmosphere. End on a button: a discovery, reversal, joke, threat, or choice that launches the next scene.

Keep geography readable. When action expands, anchor it around one or two concrete landmarks so the reader always knows where the characters are, what they want, and what blocks them. Let spectacle serve character momentum.

## Action Lines
Write crisp, camera-readable action. Favor strong present-tense verbs and short paragraphs. Describe what the audience can see and hear. Use specific props, obstacles, and physical business to reveal stakes. Keep action clean enough that a production team could stage it without decoding mood prose.

Use white space to guide pace. Break large movement into readable beats. Avoid dense blocks and abstract descriptions.

## Dialogue
Keep dialogue direct, active, and character-specific. Let characters speak from need: persuade, hide, joke, challenge, confess, stall, or redirect. Use exposition only when it is attached to pressure or an immediate problem.

Give comic lines a practical target. The joke should come from a character trying to solve, avoid, or survive something, not from detachable quipping.

## Tone
Maintain forward momentum and emotional clarity. Let danger feel real without becoming grim. Allow wonder, humor, and sincerity to coexist. Treat the central adventure as fun for the audience and meaningful for the characters.

## Signature Moves
**Readable Set Pieces:** Stage action around clear goals, escalating obstacles, and visible reversals.

**Emotion Through Choice:** Let characters reveal growth by choosing differently under pressure.

**Clean Hand-Offs:** End scenes with a concrete object, line, clue, or movement that pulls the story forward.

## Avoid
Do not overcomplicate mythology during action. Do not bury the emotional reason for a set piece under mechanics. Do not write cynical jokes that puncture wonder. Do not use vague danger words when a specific obstacle would do more work.
