## Scene Construction
Open scenes on a question, contradiction, or missing piece. Give each scene a clue function: reveal evidence, distort evidence, test a theory, expose a motive, introduce a suspect, eliminate a possibility, or reframe something the audience thought it understood.

Build scenes like mechanisms. A character enters with one assumption and leaves with a sharper but incomplete model of the truth. Let every interview, search, flashback, or discovery alter the board. End scenes with a clean turn: a new suspect, a false alibi, a hidden connection, a clue that means the opposite of what it first seemed to mean.

## Action Lines
Write observational action. Include exact details a careful viewer could remember: stains, timestamps, habits, room order, missing objects, repeated phrases, weather, labels, receipts, scuffs, or changed positions. Do not over-highlight clues. Present them naturally among other concrete details.

Use visual contrast between order and disorder. A tidy room with one wrong object can be more powerful than chaos. Keep paragraphs controlled and readable.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue as information warfare. Characters answer one question while avoiding another. Let suspects protect secrets that are not always the central crime. Use polite evasions, overly specific denials, corrected details, and contradictions between memory and evidence.

Let investigators speak in hypotheses, not declarations. The smartest character should still be revising.

## Tone
Keep curiosity active. The audience should feel invited to solve, not merely tricked. Favor elegant misdirection over random surprise. Every reveal must make prior scenes more interesting in retrospect.

## Signature Moves
**Clue in Plain Sight:** A detail appears ordinary until a later scene changes its meaning.

**Wrong Secret:** A character lies for a personal reason unrelated to the main mystery.

**Theory Collapse:** A convincing explanation fails because one small fact refuses to fit.

## Avoid
Do not introduce evidence that has no fair setup. Do not make characters withhold obvious information only because the plot needs delay. Do not use coincidence as the solution. Do not bury the emotional motive under puzzle mechanics.
