## Scene Construction
Open scenes inside ordinary social rules: a dinner, office meeting, party, neighborhood visit, family ritual, interview, school event, or polite introduction. Make the danger appear first as etiquette, expectation, coded language, exclusion, or a smile held too long.

Build scenes by forcing the protagonist to choose between self-protection and social compliance. The threat should intensify through small violations that others deny, excuse, or normalize. End scenes on a social trap: a public humiliation, invitation that cannot be refused, private warning, group consensus against the protagonist, or realization that the room has rules they do not control.

## Action Lines
Write clean, controlled dread. Use details of posture, seating, eye contact, silence, serving rituals, house rules, dress codes, locked doors, and pleasant decor with one wrong element. Keep the surface tasteful while pressure gathers underneath.

Let physical horror emerge from metaphor slowly. The social wound becomes bodily, architectural, procedural, or ritualized.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue with double meanings. Characters should sound polite, reasonable, helpful, or amused while exerting pressure. Use compliments as control, questions as accusations, jokes as warnings, and group agreement as threat.

Let the protagonist struggle to name what is wrong without sounding irrational to the room.

## Tone
Maintain controlled unease. Fear should come from being disbelieved, watched, categorized, consumed, or forced to perform comfort. Keep the horror precise and socially legible.

## Signature Moves
**Polite Threat:** A courteous line functions as a warning or command.

**Room Consensus:** Everyone else accepts the unacceptable, isolating the protagonist.

**Metaphor Turns Physical:** A social pressure becomes a visible, irreversible horror mechanism.

## Avoid
Do not make the subtext so abstract that the scene loses stakes. Do not rely only on jump scares. Do not let villains monologue the metaphor too early. Do not flatten the protagonist into a passive observer; they must keep testing exits.
