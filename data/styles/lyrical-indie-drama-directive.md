## Scene Construction
Open scenes on behavior before explanation. Let the audience enter through a chore, commute, habit, shared silence, small favor, awkward meal, or ordinary task that carries emotional residue. Give each scene a quiet pressure point rather than an obvious plot engine.

Build scenes through accumulation. Small choices, withheld reactions, repeated gestures, and half-finished conversations should reveal change. End scenes on an image, absence, interrupted confession, or action that means more than the character can say.

## Action Lines
Write with sensory restraint. Use concrete details: light through blinds, a cracked mug, a grocery bag cutting into a hand, laundry on a chair, a voicemail left unheard. Keep prose clean and unforced. Let objects carry history without overexplaining their symbolism.

Use white space for emotional weight. A pause, look away, or unfinished movement can hold more power than a paragraph of analysis.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue that circles the subject. Characters rarely say the central feeling directly until they have exhausted avoidance. Use incomplete sentences, practical questions, small corrections, and banal topics as emotional cover.

Let subtext be legible through context. The audience should feel what is being avoided without needing characters to announce it.

## Tone
Keep the emotional temperature low but charged. Avoid melodrama. Treat ordinary life as dramatic when attention is precise enough. Let humor be dry, incidental, and human.

## Signature Moves
**Meaningful Ordinary Task:** A mundane action becomes the scene's emotional argument.

**Unsaid Turn:** The scene changes because a character chooses not to say something.

**Image After Dialogue:** End on a physical detail that absorbs the conversation's damage.

## Avoid
Do not make characters explain the theme. Do not inflate small moments with poetic overstatement. Do not confuse slow pacing with inactive scenes. Do not let ambiguity erase causality; the audience should understand what changed emotionally.
