## Scene Construction
Open scenes on a problem of access: a locked room, guarded object, impossible schedule, security system, social gatekeeper, rival crew, or personal weakness that can compromise the plan. Give each scene a clean objective and a hidden contingency.

Build scenes through competence, complication, and reversal. Show the plan clearly enough that the audience can enjoy watching it bend. End scenes with a reveal: the setback was anticipated, the victory created a new risk, a team member lied, or the real target is different than expected.

## Action Lines
Write with polished momentum. Track spaces, timing, disguises, tools, screens, hands, cameras, cards, codes, elevators, service corridors, and crowd movement. Keep action elegant and readable. Use visual detail to make expertise pleasurable.

Cut between simultaneous tasks when pressure rises. Each thread should affect another thread through timing, signal, or mistake.

## Dialogue
Write dialogue as cool pressure management. Characters banter because confidence is part of the job, but each line should advance plan, character, or deception. Let specialists speak with different rhythms based on role: planner, face, tech, driver, thief, muscle, inside contact.

Use misdirection in conversation. A casual line may be instruction, cover, or test.

## Tone
Keep the experience sleek, fun, and tense. Competence should feel glamorous, but emotion should threaten the smooth surface. The crew's relationships are the plan's strongest asset and weakest link.

## Signature Moves
**Plan Overlay:** Explain a step while showing a later, messier version of it unfolding.

**Contingency Reveal:** What looked like failure becomes part of a deeper plan.

**Role Collision:** A specialist is forced outside their lane and reveals character.

## Avoid
Do not make the plan incomprehensible. Do not rely on magic technology without limits. Do not let banter stall urgency. Do not save every twist for the end; distribute reversals so momentum stays alive.
