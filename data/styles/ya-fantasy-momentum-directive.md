## Scene Construction
Open scenes with a choice, discovery, pursuit, secret, or emotional rupture already underway. Keep the story moving through revelations that change identity, loyalty, power, or danger. Each scene should sharpen the protagonist's desire and complicate the cost of getting it.

Use worldbuilding through action. Rules should appear when a character breaks, tests, fears, or needs them. End scenes with a new question, vow, betrayal, magical consequence, or romantic/emotional complication.

## Action Lines
Write vivid but clean action. Keep magical images concrete: what changes color, what burns, what lifts, what cracks, what sound fills the room. Use sensory detail, but avoid ornamental fantasy prose.

Let setting carry pressure. Schools, courts, forests, ruins, training rooms, and hidden markets should feel like systems with rules and social stakes, not decorative backdrops.

## Dialogue
Keep dialogue emotionally direct but not over-explanatory. Young characters can be sharp, defensive, funny, impulsive, and sincere in quick succession. Let attraction, rivalry, resentment, and loyalty surface through challenges and interruptions.

Use exposition as conflict: one character withholds rules, another demands answers, a third pays the price.

## Tone
Maintain urgency, wonder, and emotional intensity. Treat friendship, first love, betrayal, belonging, and self-definition as high stakes. Let mythic scale grow from personal need.

## Signature Moves
**Rule Under Pressure:** Reveal magic by forcing a character to use it badly or too soon.

**Identity Turn:** A scene changes how the protagonist understands themselves.

**Loyalty Test:** Action forces a choice between safety and attachment.

## Avoid
Do not pause for encyclopedia worldbuilding. Do not make every line sound epic. Do not overuse prophecy language. Do not let romance or mythology erase concrete scene objectives.
