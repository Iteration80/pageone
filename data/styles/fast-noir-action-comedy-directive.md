## Scene Construction
Open scenes late, with a problem already bleeding into the room. Build through confrontation, reversal, and momentum. Characters should enter with a plan, get interrupted by a worse plan, then improvise badly enough to reveal who they are.

Use action and comedy as pressure systems. A chase should expose a relationship. A joke should dodge pain. A fight should force a confession, betrayal, or reluctant act of care. End scenes on a hard turn: a punchline, threat, clue, door slam, gunshot, or wounded truth.

## Action Lines
Write lean, punchy action. Keep paragraphs short and concrete. Use vivid nouns, active verbs, and crisp visual beats. Let the prose move fast, but never become confusing. When violence erupts, track cause and effect.

Use setting as attitude: fluorescent offices, wet streets, cheap motels, holiday lights, old cars, bad coffee, broken signage. Keep details tactile and specific.

## Dialogue
Write banter as defense. Characters joke to stall, provoke, test loyalty, or hide fear. Keep exchanges fast, interruptive, and adversarial. Let dialogue snap between insult, tactic, and accidental sincerity.

Emotional admissions should feel dragged out of people by pressure, not volunteered. Let characters undercut themselves after saying something true.

## Tone
Balance cynicism with a secretly sentimental core. The world can be corrupt, loud, and bruising, but the story should still believe in loyalty, mercy, and damaged people choosing better.

## Signature Moves
**Deflection Before Truth:** A character jokes, dodges, then accidentally says the honest thing.

**Action as Argument:** Physical conflict continues a conversation characters refuse to have directly.

**Hard Comic Button:** End tense scenes on a funny, painful, or dangerous reversal.

## Avoid
Do not write interchangeable quips. Do not let cynicism smother sincerity. Do not describe coolness as a substitute for behavior. Do not make action unreadable for speed.
