## Scene Construction
Build scenes with a clear rhythmic runway. Start scenes mid-motion, with characters already engaged in high-energy activities or rapid-fire banter. Pace the progression toward musical numbers based on the type of song. For ensemble and objective numbers, accelerate the blocking, background action, and dialogue until a song is the only logical next step. For subjective ballads, drop the background noise. Isolate the character. Shift the lighting, suspend reality, or alter the environment to reflect their internal state right before the music hits.

## Action Lines
Write action blocks with a built-in, driving tempo. Use punchy, rhythmic fragments that mimic a musical beat. Describe everyday movement like choreography, even when characters are just walking down a hallway or sitting at a desk. Use capitalized verbs to emphasize kinetic beats, physical comedy, or musical cues (e.g., SLAMS the locker, SPINS, CATCHES the apple). Keep descriptions colorful, visually popping, and constantly in motion. Inject subtle physical comedy and exaggerated, highly staged reactions to emphasize the heightened theatrical reality. 

## Dialogue
Write snappy, rapid-fire banter that bounces between characters like a melody. Give the dialogue a distinct, bouncy rhythm. Inject modern wit and slight self-awareness, but never at the expense of genuine emotion. The characters must care deeply and sincerely about their goals. Use overlapping dialogue, incomplete sentences, and rhythmic interruptions to build momentum leading into ensemble songs. Keep the vocabulary accessible but theatrically sharp—lean into clever wordplay, spirited debate, and heightened declarations of feeling. 

## Tone
Maintain a baseline of infectious, high-energy joy and forward momentum. Treat teenage, young adult, or "fish-out-of-water" stakes as absolute life-or-death. Lean entirely into the earnestness of the characters' desires. Balance comedic, amphibious anarchy with sincere, sweeping theatrical emotion. Keep the world bright, slightly heightened, and endlessly kinetic. Ensure the humor is character-driven and situational, never mean-spirited.

## Signature Moves
**The Seamless Ramp-Up:** Dialogue transitions seamlessly into rhythmic chanting, clapping, or stomping, bleeding directly into the instrumental track before the first note is actually sung.
**The Spotlight Drop:** Mid-scene, reality suspends for a ballad. The bustling world freezes, slows down, or fades to black, leaving a single character in an isolated, theatrical spotlight for a subjective emotional realization.
**Choreographed Mundanity:** Ordinary actions (closing lockers, sweeping floors, passing basketballs, splashing water) are written as synchronized, rhythmic beats that establish the tempo of the scene.

## Avoid
**Do not** write cynical, detached, or overly grim dialogue; irony must never undercut the earnestness of the characters.
**Do not** write static, talking-head scenes; characters must always be moving, doing, or staging themselves physically within the space.
**Do not** drop songs in randomly without setup; never start a musical number without a rhythmic, visual, or emotional runway explicitly built into the preceding text.
**Do not** use dense, novelistic action blocks; avoid slow, meandering descriptions that kill the built-in tempo.
